The Raconteur

Rigor Mortis

A retelling of Beloved’s ‘The Four Horsemen’ scene.

Tim O’Brien
American novelist, author of The Things They Carried, guest writer at The Raconteur

Stamp Paid drove her to jail. The same woman he had ferried across the Ohio, the same woman he’d helped free, the same woman whose kid he’d fed the finest blackberries in the county—he was driving her to jail.

He’d saved the girl, yes, but it wasn’t enough. He should’ve kept his ax, he should’ve kept his ax and ran at schoolteacher the moment he saw those high-topped shoes hit the ground outside 124. He could have gotten them out, Sethe and Baby Suggs and the two girls and the two boys. But he didn’t. He couldn’t. He just stood there, staring at the shed, fingering his courage and chewing on his grit.

The truth is, all five of them in the shed were dead by the time he got there. The two boys on the floor, blood mixed with sawdust, eyes empty, mouths curved. He grabbed one of the girls, the younger one, grabbed her just as Sethe was reeling her heel back, but he didn’t save her. No. She was gone to begin with. And Sethe—poor ol’ Sethe—she was just standing there, a mess of red and black, holding on to death for dear life. They were gone—long gone—and he hadn’t been there to save ’em.

He looked at Baby Suggs, standing by the shed fanning her hands, and then at his feet, and then back at the woodpile. Just a little to the right, just a little, and maybe he’d have seen schoolteacher, seen the four horsemen ride up Bluestone Road, seen ’em hop down at 124 as if they owned the place. Why hadn’t anyone warned them? Not a single one of ’em, not a single one of ’em Blacks in Cincinnati up and moved a muscle. Stamp Paid was alone, he was alone and chewing his grit and then he swallowed it like a cough drop. The deed was done. They were gone.

A couple hours later, the sheriff forced him to drive the neighbor’s cart west into town, towards the jail. The wagon rocked gently under him, like a cradle, except the frame was full of anger and the fabric full of death. He was driving Sethe to jail. He closed his eyes.
